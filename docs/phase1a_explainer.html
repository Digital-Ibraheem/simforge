1 · The 60-second summary

You just trained a neural network to control a simulated robot arm using reinforcement learning. Here's what happened at the highest level:

1
A robot arm spawned in MuJoCo physics simulation

MuJoCo created a virtual world: a Fetch robot arm, a floating red target sphere, gravity, joints with motors. Every physics tick is 2ms of simulated time.

2
The arm flailed randomly for the first ~1,000 steps

Before learning starts, the agent takes random actions to fill the replay buffer with diverse experience. You can think of this as "wandering around to build a map before navigating."

3
SAC trained two neural networks simultaneously

An actor (the policy — "given what I see, what should I do?") and a critic (the value function — "how good is this state?"). They improved each other in a loop.

4
HER relabeled failed episodes as fake successes

Every time the arm went to the wrong place, HER asked "what if that was the goal?" and added that relabeled experience to training. This created a flood of learning signal even before the arm ever reached the real target.

5
By ~20,000 steps the arm was reliably reaching the target

The policy converged to 100% success rate. It learned: "move gripper toward the red sphere." Took 3.5 minutes on CPU.

Why this matters
This exact pipeline — SAC + HER — is what makes Phase 1b (pick-and-place) possible. The only difference is the task is 10× harder (requires grasping), so it takes hours instead of minutes.

2 · The task: FetchReach-v4

FetchReach is the simplest task in the Fetch robotics benchmark. A Fetch robot arm must move its gripper to a randomly-placed target position in 3D space. No object, no grasping — just reach.

Observation space

At each timestep, the agent receives a dictionary with three keys:

KeyShapeWhat it contains
"observation" Box(10,) [0:3] gripper position (x,y,z in meters)
[3:6] gripper linear velocity
[6:8] finger positions (left, right)
[8:10] finger velocities
"achieved_goal" Box(3,) Where the gripper tip currently is (same as obs[0:3])
"desired_goal" Box(3,) Where the gripper needs to go (the red sphere position)
Why a dictionary observation?
Goal-conditioned tasks need the agent to know both where it is and where it needs to go. Using a dict with achieved_goal and desired_goal as separate keys is what allows HER to work — it can swap out the goal without touching the rest of the observation. This is why we use MultiInputPolicy instead of MlpPolicy.

Action space

Box(-1, 1, shape=(4,)) — four continuous values:

  • action[0:3] — displacement velocity for the gripper in x, y, z
  • action[3] — gripper open/close (negative = close, positive = open)

Actions are clipped to [-1, 1]. A value of 1.0 means "move at maximum speed in that direction." The arm has 7 degrees of freedom internally, but the action space is in Cartesian space (the controller handles the inverse kinematics).

Reward function

Sparse: r = 0 if the gripper is within 5cm of the target, r = -1 otherwise. Every step of every episode returns -1 until success.

Why sparse reward is hard
With 50 steps per episode and random exploration, the probability of randomly landing within 5cm of a random 3D point is tiny. Without HER, the agent would receive -1 for thousands of episodes with zero learning signal. Standard Q-learning or policy gradient would barely move off random behavior.

3 · How SAC works

SAC (Soft Actor-Critic) is an off-policy, model-free RL algorithm designed for continuous action spaces. "Off-policy" means it can learn from experience collected by older versions of the policy — it stores all past experience in a replay buffer and reuses it.

The two networks

NetworkInput → OutputJob
Actor (policy) π observation → action distribution Decides what to do. Outputs a Gaussian distribution over actions — a mean and std for each of the 4 action dimensions. During training: sample from it. During eval: use the mean.
Critic (value fn) Q (observation, action) → scalar value Estimates "how much future reward will we get if we take this action now?" Used to give the actor a learning signal. SAC uses two critics (Q1, Q2) and takes the minimum to avoid overestimation.

The training loop (every step after 1,000 warmup)

1
Collect experience

Actor samples an action from π(s), steps the environment, gets (s, a, r, s'). Store in replay buffer (capacity: 1M transitions).

2
Sample a batch from the replay buffer

256 random transitions, mixing old and new experience. Old data is valid because SAC is off-policy.

3
Update the critics

Compute the Bellman target: r + γ · min(Q1, Q2)(s', π(s')). Train Q1 and Q2 to predict this target via MSE loss. This is critic_loss.

4
Update the actor

Train the actor to maximize Q(s, a) − α·log π(a|s). The second term is entropy — it rewards the policy for staying uncertain/exploratory. This is actor_loss.

5
Update the temperature α (ent_coef)

α controls how much entropy matters. SAC auto-tunes it. If the policy is too deterministic, α increases. If too random, α decreases. This is ent_coef_loss.

6
Soft-update target networks (tau=0.05)

Target networks are slowly-moving copies of the critics used in step 3. target = (1-τ)·target + τ·online. They stabilize training — without them, the Bellman target would shift wildly every step.

Why SAC for robotics?
SAC's entropy maximization is particularly valuable for manipulation: it discourages the policy from collapsing to a single deterministic strategy early in training. This keeps the agent exploring different grasping approaches, which matters a lot for pick-and-place where there are multiple valid ways to grasp an object.

4 · How HER works

HER (Hindsight Experience Replay) is not a separate algorithm — it's a technique layered on top of SAC's replay buffer. It solves the sparse reward problem by manufacturing learning signal from failed episodes.

The core insight

What actually happened
Goal: [0.8, 0.5, 0.4]
Arm ended at: [0.6, 0.3, 0.4]
Reward: all -1s
Result: useless? ✗
→
HER relabels
After HER relabeling
Goal: [0.6, 0.3, 0.4]
Arm ended at: [0.6, 0.3, 0.4]
Reward: …-1, -1, 0
Result: success! ✓

HER takes a failed episode and asks: "What if the goal had been wherever the arm actually went?" It adds this relabeled version to the replay buffer alongside the original. Now the agent has successfully achieved something, giving it real gradient signal to learn from.

Goal selection strategy: "future"

The goal_selection_strategy="future" setting means: for each transition at step t in an episode, relabel using goals achieved at a random future timestep t' > t in the same episode. This is empirically the best strategy because:

  • The relabeled goal is reachable (it was actually reached later in the episode)
  • The transitions leading up to it get meaningful reward shaping
  • "Future" goals are near in state space, so the value estimates are accurate

n_sampled_goal = 4

For each real transition stored in the buffer, HER generates 4 relabeled versions. So for every 1 real experience, you get 4 artificial successes. This amplifies the training signal by 4× with no extra environment steps.

How HER is implemented in SB3
HerReplayBuffer wraps the normal replay buffer. When SAC calls buffer.sample(256), HER automatically mixes real and relabeled transitions. From SAC's perspective, it's just getting a bigger, richer batch. The relabeling happens transparently.

7 · Every metric explained

success_rate
1.0
Fraction of episodes where info["is_success"]=True — the gripper was within 5cm of target when the episode ended. The only metric that actually matters.
ep_rew_mean
-1.68
Mean total reward per episode. With sparse reward (-1/step) and 50-step episodes: if the agent succeeds at step 48, reward = -48. Not -50 because HER relabeled some.
ep_len_mean
50
Always 50 here — every episode runs to the max step limit. The sparse reward doesn't terminate early on success; it just gives 0 instead of -1.
fps
234
Environment steps per second. 234 fps is good for CPU-only MuJoCo. Pick-and-place will be similar — the simulation step cost is the bottleneck, not the neural network.
actor_loss
variable
Unlike supervised learning, actor loss oscillating (positive and negative) is normal and expected. It's not MSE — it's the negative expected Q-value plus entropy, not a "lower is better" number.
critic_loss
0.02
MSE between Q-value prediction and Bellman target. This should trend downward. Very low (~0.02) means the critic accurately predicts returns — it "understands" the task.
ent_coef (α)
0.0015
The entropy temperature. Started around 1.0, collapsed to ~0.001 as the policy converged. A near-zero α means SAC stopped encouraging exploration — the policy is confident and deterministic.
n_updates
48,999
Total gradient updates to the neural networks. With train_freq=1, gradient_steps=1, there's one update per environment step after warmup.
Common confusion: actor_loss going negative
In supervised learning, loss going negative would mean something is broken. In SAC, actor_loss = −E[Q(s,a)] + α·H(π), which can be negative when Q-values are large. Don't use actor_loss to judge training health. Use success_rate and critic_loss instead.

from stable_baselines3 import SAC
import gymnasium as gym
import gymnasium_robotics  # noqa

model = SAC.load("results/models/reach/sac_her_reach_s42/best/best_model")
env = gym.make("FetchReach-v4", render_mode="human")  # "human" opens a window

obs, _ = env.reset()
for _ in range(200):
    action, _ = model.predict(obs, deterministic=True)
    obs, reward, terminated, truncated, info = env.step(action)
    if terminated or truncated:
        obs, _ = env.reset()

env.close()

10 · Phase 1b: FetchPickAndPlace — what the task required

Pick-and-place is fundamentally harder than reach because it requires a sequential skill chain. The agent has to discover and chain all of these in the right order using only sparse reward:

1
Move gripper above the block

Use obs[6:9] (block relative to gripper) to home in. HER teaches this early — any episode where the arm got near the block is relabeled as "reached the block goal."

2
Lower to block height and close fingers

The hardest step to discover via exploration. The gripper must close (action[3] < 0) at exactly the right moment — too early misses, too late knocks the block away. This is why success_rate stayed at 0% until ~200k steps.

3
Lift the block against gravity

Once grasped, the arm must move upward while keeping fingers closed. The block's weight applies force to the finger joints — the policy learns to increase grip force to compensate.

4
Transport to target and release

Navigate 3D space to the desired_goal position while holding the block. Open fingers (action[3] > 0) when within tolerance. The release timing matters — too early drops it before the goal.

The observation space (25-dim)

IndicesMeaningWhy it matters
[0:3]Gripper position (x,y,z)Where the arm is in world space
[3:6]Block position (x,y,z)Where the block is
[6:9]Block relative to gripperThe approach vector — most important for grasping
[9:11]Finger positionsHow open/closed the gripper is
[11:14]Block rotation (Euler)Orientation — matters for non-symmetric objects
[14:17]Block linear velocityIs the block moving? Is it falling?
[17:20]Block angular velocityIs the block spinning?
[20:23]Gripper linear velocityHow fast the arm is moving
[23:25]Finger velocityAre fingers opening or closing?

The silent phase (0–200k steps) — what was actually happening

We checked the eval log at 200k steps and saw all rewards between -50 and -45:

[(10000, -50.0), (20000, -50.0), (30000, -47.5), (40000, -45.0),
 (50000, -50.0), ... (200000, -50.0)]

-50.0 = failed every step of every episode (50 steps × -1 = -50). -47.5 = maybe 2–3 steps where the arm happened to be within 5cm of the goal — almost certainly noise, not real grasping. Success rate was effectively 0%.

But the agent wasn't doing nothing during this time. HER was building up rich experience of moving toward the block (relabeled as "successfully reached block position"). The policy was learning the approach geometry. Grasping discovery is a threshold event — sudden, not gradual.

Why ep_rew_mean = -10.2 even at 99% success?
With sparse reward, every step costs -1. If the agent succeeds at step 10, it collects 10 × (-1) = -10 reward for that episode, plus 40 × 0 = 0 for remaining steps. 13 · What's next: Phase 2 — Domain Randomization

Phase 1 is complete. You have a 99%-success baseline policy trained on standard physics. The next step is building the domain randomization system and training three ablation variants to answer: does randomizing physics during training make the policy more robust to new physics?

The three ablation variants

VariantConfigWhat it randomizesHypothesis
No DR dr_off.yaml Nothing — default physics every episode High in-distribution, fragile to any physics change
Moderate DR dr_moderate.yaml Mass ±20%, friction ±20%, gravity ±0.3 m/s², obs noise 5mm, 1-step delay Slight in-dist drop, much better OOD
Aggressive DR dr_aggressive.yaml Mass ±50–100%, friction ±70%, gravity ±1.5 m/s², obs noise 15mm, 2-step delay Lower in-dist, best OOD — but may over-regularize

What the DR wrapper does under the hood

Every time the environment resets for a new episode, DomainRandomizationWrapper.reset():

  1. Calls the real env's reset() (restores simulator to clean state)
  2. Samples new random values for each parameter (mass multiplier, friction multiplier, etc.)
  3. Writes them directly into the MuJoCo mjModel arrays (model.body_mass[id] = new_mass)
  4. Calls mujoco.mj_setConst(model, data) to recompute cached inertia constants

The policy never sees the physics values directly — it only sees observations. It must learn to infer physics from behavior and act robustly across all of them.

Run the DR tests first
Before training 9 ablation runs (~12 hours total), verify the DR wrapper works: 
pytest tests/ -v
Then start the no-DR ablation variant (so all 3 ablation runs use the same hyperparams and seeds, making the comparison fair): 
python scripts/03_ablation.py --variant no_dr --no-wandb
